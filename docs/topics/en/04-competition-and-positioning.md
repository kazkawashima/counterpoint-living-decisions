# 04 — Competition, Positioning, and Naming

Sources: `talk5.md`, `talk8.md`, `talk9.md`, `talk11.md`, and `talk2.md`.

> **Important:** Category submission counts were not public at the time of the
> conversations. The shares and competition rates below are estimates from the
> `talk5` branch, not facts. The `talk11` branch explicitly left the claim that
> Education was less crowded as an unverified hypothesis.

## Category competition

Recorded registrant counts were approximately 29,000 in `talk5` and 32,000 in
another `talk11` statement. These are time-bound observations, not a current
count. Category submission counts were unknown because the gallery was not
public.

| Category | Estimated share in `talk5` | Estimated least crowded | Reason and weakness | Confidence |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| Work & Productivity | 30–38% | ~5% | Generic business AI is likely concentrated here | Low; submissions unverified |
| Apps for Your Life | 25–33% | ~10% | Easy entry may increase submissions | Low; submissions unverified |
| Developer Tools | 18–25% | next-most sparse, ~25% | Fewer entries but possibly higher technical density | Low |
| Education | 12–20% | least crowded, ~60% | Hypothesis that domain knowledge reduces entry | **Low; unverified in `talk11`** |

The recorded strategy inference is that equal prizes may create an incentive to
choose a sparse category, but sparsity itself is unconfirmed. Problem
understanding, completeness, and demo differentiation matter more than raw
submission-count estimates.

### Candidate category mapping

| Concept | Closest category |
|---|---|
| Counterpoint / Living Decisions / meeting decisions | Work & Productivity is the natural fit |
| Promise Compiler (promise → acceptance test) | **Developer Tools** |
| Misconception diagnosis + AI student | **Education** |
| Broad Concept A exploration | Work, but submission-kill candidate |
| CommitLayer | Work, but submission-kill candidate |

Judge by primary user, primary problem, and most frequent use case—not simply by
the format of the input.

## Winning frame

> **Obvious pain × non-obvious mechanism**

The recorded criteria were:

1. It cannot be derived directly from release notes.
2. It first seems strange, then inevitable after explanation.
3. It can be explained in three minutes.
4. A non-specialist can judge success from the screen.

“Multi-agent for X” is likely to overlap with many entries. In the recorded
tie-break framing, a sufficiently non-obvious mechanism with a complete
experience beats a more novel idea with a rough demo.

## Naming and message hierarchy

One name and one tagline cannot land equally with everyone. Use a hierarchy:

| Layer | Role | Counterpoint candidate |
|---|---|---|
| Product name | Memory | Counterpoint |
| Category descriptor | What it is | The commitment layer for agent-native teams |
| Core tagline | Worldview | Independent minds. Shared commitment. |
| Judge hook | OpenAI-facing | When everyone has an agent, the room needs a protocol. |
| Competitive contrast | Difference | Meeting AI records the room. Counterpoint changes what the room can decide. |
| Living Decisions hook | Time axis | Decisions should know when they are no longer true. |

Avoid collision-prone names such as Quorum, Resolve, Convene, Pact, and Accord.
`commitment layer` and `Decision Runtime` fit the MVP better than `Decision OS`
or `Meeting OS`.

> A meeting is an interface, not a product category. (`talk8`)
